Remote interaction service: Watch2Gether.

People may no longer watch America's Funniest Home Videos nowadays but people share funny clips with their friends and family more than ever in the digital age. With social interaction ever so limited during the pandemic, W2G.tv offers a service of watching video clips in real-time. For our project, we examine the current UX of such remote interaction.

By Peyton Baur and Harrison Huh

Next: Contextual Inquiry

Contextual Inquiry

We performed two instances of contextual inquiry, with a total of 5 people. To probe the remote interaction authentically, we watched the users through shared screens on Zoom. As they carried out a handful of tasks that covers the key features of the site, we observed their site interaction carefully and asked questions.

Create Room & Share Invite Link

The first step to the remote interaction is the initiative of creating a room and sharing an invite link to people. While the users did not have any trouble making a new room, they had some trouble with sharing the invite link. Locating and copying the invite link was not difficult as the users were able to find the yellow '+ Invite friends' button on the top right. However, it was sending out the link itself that gave them trouble. The users were on their work computer, thus they did not have a messaging app available right away to share the link. One user immediately turned to Gmail to share the link while the other manually typed the link on their phone in a messaging app.

Adding Videos to Playlist

Submitting a video to the playlist is a crucial interaction to the process. However, to the first time users, the task was not as obvious as it should be. On the very top screen, there is the search bar where a user can enter keywords to search YouTube videos and add videos to the playlist. One of our users commented that he did “not see any button to add a video to playlist” and struggled to figure out how to add a video directly on the w2g.tv site. Two of our users did not use the search bar immediately and instead searched the videos on YouTube itself and copied the video links. After acquiring the links to their desired video, they did not recognize that the top search bar was the place to add videos. After minutes of struggle, they finally inserted the links to the search bar, which returned the video as a search result at the bottom of the screen below the video screen. Only then were they able to add their clips to the list by clicking the '+' button. After finally adding a video, one user remarked that it was “hard for [her] to tell what the function of anything on the website was."

Playlist & Changing Videos

On the right of the video are the playlist and history. As the users added their videos, they were able to confirm right away that a new video was added to the queue. Most notably, when the users were searching for a place to add a video, the users looked into the playlist immediately. It is possible that the users not only expected the video adding feature to be within the playlist section but also some of the interfaces on the playlist are misleading. When prompted to skipping or replaying videos, the users were able to easily click on the desired video.

Watching Together

While the users were able to add and switch videos, they seemed to have a small impression on the experience of watching together. One user clicked on a video in the playlist without realizing that the video changed for all the other users. The other user was in a wrong room, but did not realize it even when other users were adding videos. The entire experience was barely different from watching on YouTube itself, as one user would say. Another user commented that the video player should be more in line with the standard buttons and icons available on other platforms. Furthermore, when entering full screen mode, one user was frustrated that she could "not see the chat or playlist anymore," claiming that this "somewhat defeats the purpose of the site" because once in full screen, she could no longer interact with other users.

Next we built a model to wholly capture our user research.

Model

Model

Aside from the initial step of the room creation and invitation, the workflow of each user is identical once the users get to the core features of videos and chat. We decided to make a sequential model on how easily the users were able to carry out the desired tasks. Within the sequential model, we divided it into several sections of the key features of using the site.

Next we use the model to brainstorm and design a solution.

Brainstorm

Key Takeaways from the Model

Looking at the key issues in our model, we brainstormed how to find solutions for them.

  • In the case where direct links are inaccessible, make a room code that's short enough to manually type in.
  • Place the video search/add functionality closer to the playlist since it seems to be the spot where most users look to.
  • Separate the search feature and add feature separately so users can efficiently do either of them.
  • Place the search/add GUI away from the screen so the video is still visible on screen.
  • Make fullscreen mode available on the video control bar.
  • In the fullscreen mode, allow access to key features such as chat and add/search.
  • Add the react emoji option for interaction.
  • Make the anonymous usernames more readable and make the user list bigger.

    • Main Hub

    In the main hub, there are clear options for creating a room and joining a room. In the orignal website, there were no option to join a room as they only rely on URL for joining. Here, we put the option of joining a room right below creating a room.

    Joining a room

    If the user wants to join a room and they do not have the link, they can enter a 5-letter room code. This was designed to combat an issue where users would need extra steps to share a code if sharing the link is not directly possible.

    Invitation

    Now the site has two options of sharing a room, offering both link and a 5-letter room code. This is directly seen when a user creates a room as that is the first task most users do when they create a room.

    The Room

    This is the main view of users being in a room together. The user list is bigger at the bottom to increase their presence and the anonymous temporary usernames are more recognizable as opposed to random letters assigned to them before. The features of searching/adding videos are also moved on top of the playlist.

    Search & Add

    The search feature is now limited to the side, allowing the video to be unobstructed while the user quickly searches for a video using keywords.

    Direct Link

    An option to add videos via direct links is more explicit as well. It also does not obstruct the video nor the chat.

    Fullscreen Mode

    The fullscreen mode is clearly visible on the control bar of the video. While on fullscreen, the control offers options that were unavailable beforehand.

    Fullscreen Playlist

    While in the fullscreen, the user can press the 5th button from the right to have a quick view of the playlist without obstructing the entire video.

    Fullscreen Chat

    The user can also access the chat as well, using the 4th button from the right.

    Reacting with Emoji

    The user also has the option to react with emojis using the 6th button from the right. When used, reactions are displayed on screen for the users in the room.

    Prototype Evaluation

    Prototype Evaluation

    We had our users from the contextual inquiry review the prototype video and give us feedback. We asked these questions to see if we had successfully solved any of the issues from the original inquiry and if there is any other room for improvement.

    Can you see yourself using the product as shown in the video?

    “I think so. It seems like a good way to watch videos with your friends since we can’t see each other in person really.”

    “I probably would have never used the original site because it was so frustrating, but this seems like it would be easier to use so if I wanted to watch YouTube with friends, I would use it.”

    "Yes. This isn't a product I would use, but I think that the demo showed how it could be used"

    Are there any other features on the website you wanted to see that wasn’t shown in the video?

    “I think maybe adapting the video chatting interface to be shown in full screen but that might’ve been beyond scope”

    “No I don’t think so”

    "I don't think so, it's a pretty simple and barebones product"

    Were you able to understand all the clicks and intentions in the video? Specify if any part of the video confused you.

    “Yes”

    “Some parts were a little awkward with the playing of the video but that’s more due to video-editing ability”

    "I think that it could be clearer if you add a visual indication to where you're clicking, since the cursor is small. Like add a red circle, or an arrow, something like that to show where clicks are being registered"

    Any other thoughts?

    “I think this is a lot more clear, it removed a lot of the clutter on the original site and it is quite obvious how to add videos to your playlist. I also like how the chat is like more obvious now.”

    “I like the redesign a lot. I think that being able to view the chat and playlist while in full screen creates a more immersive experience. I think the idea of emoji reactions is cool too”

    Conclusion

    Conclusion

    Looking over the user evaluations, it seems our prototype has successfully demonstrated the use of the product, even having a better impression than the original occasionally. Perhaps the video prototype could have added the demo of the chat feature in full-screen mode but aside from that, we believe the video was mostly clear to the users. Given our limited time with editing the video, the user had mild confusion about the YouTube clip playing in the video; however, we do not see it as an issue to our prototype itself. Overall, our prototype has gotten positive feedback, especially regarding the clarity of feature presence and usage.
